You don't ship pages anymore. You ship the grammar.

The user's intent picks the surface, not the team's guess. A developer's view of what comes after the hand-authored page.

A developer's manifesto  ·  2026 Resonance · Elixir + Phoenix LiveView
02 · the problem

Every page in your app is a bet.

Someone on the team thought: a user in this situation will need this view, in this layout, with these affordances, so the team built that page. Most pages get traffic; some don't.

The good guesses get used; the bad guesses become dead-link archaeology. This worked when the cost of misreading a user was an unused screen. It works less well now that the cost is invisibility: the user opens your app, doesn't see what they came for, and switches to a tool that gets out of the way.

Premise 01
Pages were one expression of the product. Not the product.
03 · the surface beneath the page

The real surface area was already there.

Underneath every page is the data the application owns, the operations it permits, the constraints those operations honor, the auth scopes that gate them, and the UI patterns the team has chosen to render results.

Take any noun in your app. A Deal probably has a list view, a show view, an edit form, and a few special-case pages like ArchiveStaleDealsLive. Each is a bet on which slice of the same data will get used, pre-bundled into a route before the user shows up.

Pages don't get harder to write. They stop being the unit of work. The unit of work becomes the grammar itself: the declared surface area, written once, against which any plan can compile.

04 · the architectural shift

Insert a typed plan between intent and surface.

▸ intent to surface 1 opaque · the rest glass 
user intent
  → planner (LLM)               [opaque]
    → WorkspacePlan             [glass · typed value]
      → validation              [glass · against the Manifest]
        → compile               [glass · deterministic]
          → Renderables         [glass · stable identity]
            → Phoenix renders the surface

One step is opaque: the planner. It sits in the position a query optimizer sits in a SQL database; a learned mechanism doing the matching, surrounded by glass on both sides. Everything downstream is deterministic. The plan is a value you can store, diff, and rerun tomorrow without a new model call.

Premise 02
Resonance composes the surface.
Phoenix renders it.
05 · the substrate stays

The data layer stays. The surface gets generated.

CRUD is still the base calculus: Create, Read, Update, Delete, over resources owned by Phoenix contexts, persisted by Ecto, gated by authorization scopes you wrote. Nothing in that stack is replaced.

What changes is who decides which CRUD operation gets the user's screen real estate at this moment.

Domain verbs like archive_deal, refund_invoice, approve_request, or escalate_ticket are named compositions: CRUD plus constraints plus confirmations plus patterns. The substrate is unchanged; the addressability changes.

07 · resources & read shapes

Nouns the planner sees. Verbs it's allowed to ask.

I · Resources

The product-meaningful nouns.

Not necessarily one-to-one with database tables. StaleDeal and AtRiskRenewal may be named read shapes over the same Deal table. Each resource declares the fields safe and meaningful to expose, which is usually narrower than the schema's full surface.

Schemas describe rows. Resources describe meaning.

II · Read shapes

Five typed primitives.

  • rank_entities · who's largest, fastest, most stuck
  • segment_population · how the set breaks down
  • compare_over_time · trends and deltas
  • show_distribution · shape of a measure across a dimension
  • summarize_findings · prose summary over a result

A plan that asks against measures the app never declared fails at validation.

08 · mutation shapes & constraints

Boundaries the planner can't cross.

III · Mutation shapes

CRUD, but explicit.

Each mutation declares allowed parameters, validations, authorization scope, confirmation requirements, reversibility, side effects, and after-state. Your changesets and contexts already encode most of this. The Manifest doesn't bypass them; it advertises them.

Read workspaces are proven today. Mutation surfaces are the next frontier.

IV · Constraints

The operating envelope.

Changeset validations. Database constraints. Authorization policies. Tenant scope. Idempotency. Audit requirements. Confirmations. Irreversibility gates.

The old web's hidden skeleton, made explicit. They keep the model from planning outside what your app actually supports.

09 · patterns & external capabilities

What can render. What can be admitted.

V · Patterns

The model picks a pattern. Not HTML.

  • record_list · scannable rows with selection
  • detail_panel · one record, one focus
  • bar_chart, trend_panel · shape-bearing surfaces
  • metric_strip, prose_summary · context-bearing surfaces

The compiler binds the pattern to your function components and LiveComponents.

VI · External capabilities

Admitted, not auto-flowed.

MCP tools, HTTP endpoints, SDK calls, background jobs: any capability your app uses. Each is admitted into the grammar one at a time, with an annotation that says which product operation it implements.

A capability the app hasn't admitted does not exist as far as the planner is concerned.

10 · the manifest in code

A Manifest you could read on a napkin.

Illustrative, not the final API. The shape of what a developer authors once.

▸ manifest.ex  ·  CRM grammar fragment elixir 
# Resource: the nouns the planner sees
resource :deal do
  fields do
    field :name,        :string
    field :stage,       :enum, values: [:qualified, :proposal, :negotiation, :closed]
    field :owner_id,    :user_ref
    field :value_cents, :money
    field :closed_at,   :datetime, nullable: true
  end

  measures   [:count, :sum_value, :avg_value]
  dimensions [:stage, :owner, :closed_at, :region]
  filters    [:stage, :owner, :closed_at]
end

# Operation: CRUD with declared boundaries
operation :archive_deals do
  crud      :update
  resource  :deal
  updates   [:archived_at]

  accepts_many           true
  requires_confirmation  true
  reversible_with        :restore_deals

  validate_with CRM.Deals, :archive_changeset
  perform_with  CRM.Deals, :archive_deals
end

# Admitted primitives and patterns
primitives [:rank_entities, :segment_population,
            :compare_over_time, :summarize_findings]

patterns   [:bar_chart, :trend_panel, :entity_list,
            :data_table, :prose_summary]
11 · MCP and the missing P

MCP gets you the wire. MPC gets you the surface.

MCP, the Model Context Protocol, is a standard worth using. It solves one specific problem: how a tool exposes itself to a model. Resources, tools, prompts, catalog-style handshakes. We're for it.

MCP is the wire format for "here is a tool you can call." It is not a product grammar. A model that can plan a safe, app-native surface needs more: which operations require confirmation, which patterns can render which results, what the audit trail looks like, what the after-state should show.

M
Manifest
What the app has declared it can do. Resources, reads, mutations, constraints, patterns, admitted capabilities.
P
Plan
What this user needs, this moment. A typed value the planner emits, validated against the Manifest.
C
Compile
The deterministic step from plan to surface. Plan in, Renderables out. Phoenix mounts.
Premise 03
If MCP standardizes how capabilities are exposed,
MPC standardizes how capabilities become safe surfaces.
12 · two pictures

A tool picker is not a product surface.

The wrong picture

Pick. Call. Render.

user intent → model picks MCP tool → tool runs

A demo. A parlor trick with an API key.

The right picture

Plan. Validate. Preview. Execute.

user intent → plan → validation against admitted capabilities → preview or confirmation → app-owned execution → result + audit

A product surface.

13 · what Phoenix becomes

Phoenix doesn't change. Its parts get repurposed.

Contexts

The operation boundary.

Still the right place for CRM.Deals.archive_deals/2 and Billing.Invoices.refund_invoice/3. The Manifest points at context functions. Schemas and LiveView events are left alone.

LiveView

Runtime host for planned workspaces.

A route like /workspace doesn't ship pre-decided sections; it shows whatever the plan compiled into. Lifecycle, PubSub, URL state, auth re-checks: Phoenix owns it.

Components

Pattern implementations.

Function components and LiveComponents become how a pattern is realized. The planner picks record_list; the app decides how record_list becomes Phoenix code.

Routes

Entry points for hardened workspaces.

They don't disappear. They become stable points for hosts, saved workspaces, canonical resources. Not every useful surface needs a route before a user can ask for it.

14 · a day building a feature

"Show me at-risk renewals for the next 60 days, grouped by owner."

The page-centric day

Anticipation is the bottleneck.

  • Every variation on the same noun gets its own screen.
  • Someone has to predict the variation before the user asks.
  • Routes accumulate; so does the maintenance bill.
  • The user finds the right screen, or doesn't.
The planned-surface day

The grammar already covers it.

  • renewals is a declared resource.
  • at_risk is a declared filter.
  • rank_entities and summarize_findings already work over admitted measures.
  • entity_list and detail_panel already render this shape.
  • The user asks. The plan compiles. Done.

When the grammar doesn't cover it, you author once and unlock every future workspace.

15 · the trade

Implementation flexibility for declarative leverage.

Easier

The long tail. Free.

  • The long tail. Workspaces a user wants but aren't worth a page-week of effort.
  • New surfaces over old data. The expense moves from building the view to getting the dimension declared cleanly.
  • Removing dead surfaces. Routes you no longer need don't have to be maintained because they don't exist.
  • Onboarding. The Manifest is one artifact a new engineer reads to understand the app.
Harder

Discipline shifts up.

  • Declarative discipline. Vague resource names used to surface as LiveView bugs. Now they surface as plans you wouldn't have written.
  • Cross-surface invariants. A caveat that used to live in one template now has to live in a constraint or pattern.
  • Performance reasoning. A planner can compose patterns that hit your resolver harder than any hand-authored page would have.
  • Pattern vs primitive. Precision you used to defer becomes precision the Manifest forces.

The surface area of your app comes to reflect what people actually do with it, rather than what the team predicted at planning time.
